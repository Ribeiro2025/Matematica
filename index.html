Homework 1: Sequences and Series

Here's an example of how mathematical formulas will render:

Theorem 1: For a convergent sequence $\{a_n\}$, if $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$, then for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists an $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \geq N$: $$|a_n - L| < \epsilon$$
Problem 1: Prove that the sequence defined by: $$a_n = \frac{n^2 + 1}{n^2 - 1}$$ converges as $n \to \infty$, and find its limit.

Solution: Let's solve this step by step:

1) First, let's try to simplify this expression: $$\frac{n^2 + 1}{n^2 - 1} = \frac{n^2 + 1}{n^2 - 1} \cdot \frac{1/n^2}{1/n^2} = \frac{1 + 1/n^2}{1 - 1/n^2}$$ 2) As $n \to \infty$, $\frac{1}{n^2} \to 0$, therefore: $$\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \frac{1 + 0}{1 - 0} = 1$$
